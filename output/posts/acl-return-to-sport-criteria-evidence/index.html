ACL Return-to-Sport Testing: Which Criteria Actually Predict Re-Injury Risk

After ACL reconstruction, the decision to clear an athlete for sport return remains one of the most challenging calls in physiotherapy. Despite decades of research and evolving protocols, re-injury rates hover around 15-25%, suggesting our current testing batteries may not be identifying the athletes truly at risk.

The problem lies not in the absence of testing criteria, but in distinguishing which measures actually predict future injury from those that simply reassure clinicians. Recent systematic reviews reveal a stark disconnect between commonly used tests and their ability to identify athletes heading for re-injury.

Time-Based Clearance Falls Short

The traditional approach of clearing athletes at 6-9 months post-surgery, regardless of functional capacity, has largely been abandoned by evidence-based practitioners. Research consistently demonstrates that time alone is a poor predictor of readiness, with significant variation in recovery trajectories between individuals.1

More concerning is the finding that many athletes who meet time-based criteria still demonstrate significant bilateral deficits in strength, power, and movement quality. A systematic review of return-to-sport protocols found that athletes cleared purely on timeline often returned with limb symmetry indices below 85% - a threshold associated with increased re-injury risk.2

The shift toward criterion-based progression has gained momentum, but implementation remains inconsistent across practices. Many clinicians continue to use time as the primary factor, supplemented by subjective assessments that lack the sensitivity to detect meaningful deficits.3

Strength Testing: Beyond Simple Symmetry

Isokinetic strength testing remains the gold standard for ACL return-to-sport assessment, but the interpretation requires more nuance than simple limb symmetry indices suggest. While the 90% symmetry threshold is widely cited, research indicates this may be insufficient for high-risk athletes.

The Delaware-Oslo ACL cohort study, tracking over 200 athletes, found that combining quadriceps strength (≥90% symmetry) with hop test performance and psychological readiness reduced re-injury risk by 84%.6 Crucially, athletes meeting only strength criteria without functional and psychological components showed no significant reduction in re-injury rates.

Peak torque measurements, while important, may miss critical deficits in rate of force development and endurance capacity. Athletes can achieve acceptable peak strength while demonstrating significant deficits in explosive power - a key component of sport-specific movements and injury prevention mechanisms.

For practices without isokinetic equipment, handheld dynamometry provides a practical alternative, though standardisation of testing positions and protocols becomes critical for meaningful interpretation.

Functional Testing That Predicts Risk

Hop testing protocols have evolved from simple single-leg hop distances to comprehensive batteries assessing multiple movement planes and demands. However, not all hop tests demonstrate equal predictive validity for re-injury risk.

The four-hop protocol (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, and 6-metre timed hop) provides the most robust assessment when combined with qualitative movement analysis. Research indicates that achieving ≥90% symmetry across all four tests, while maintaining good movement quality, significantly reduces re-injury likelihood.9

Movement quality assessment during hop testing may be more predictive than distance or time measures alone. Athletes demonstrating dynamic knee valgus, trunk lateral flexion, or poor landing mechanics face increased risk regardless of quantitative performance. Standardised scoring systems for movement quality help objectify these observations.

Recently validated assessments incorporate sport-specific demands more effectively than traditional hop protocols. These include change-of-direction tests, reactive agility assessments, and sport-specific movement patterns that better replicate the demands athletes will face upon return.12

Psychological Readiness: The Missing Component

Psychological factors may be the strongest predictor of successful sport return, yet they remain the most neglected component of return-to-sport testing. Fear of re-injury, reduced confidence, and kinesiophobia directly impact movement patterns and decision-making during sport participation.

The ACL-Return to Sport after Injury (ACL-RSI) scale provides a validated tool for assessing psychological readiness. Athletes scoring below 76 on this scale demonstrate significantly higher rates of re-injury and reduced performance levels upon return.13

Psychological readiness cannot be rushed or trained in isolation from physical preparation. The most effective protocols integrate confidence-building exercises throughout rehabilitation, progressing from controlled environments to increasingly complex, sport-like scenarios.

Athletes may achieve excellent physical test scores while remaining psychologically unprepared for return. This disconnect often manifests as altered movement patterns under pressure, reduced aggressiveness in competition, and ultimately increased injury risk through compensatory behaviors.

Integration and Decision-Making

The most predictive approach combines multiple domains rather than relying on any single criterion. The evidence suggests a hierarchical model where biological healing provides the foundation, physical capacity enables function, and psychological readiness determines successful sport engagement.14

Minimum thresholds should include: quadriceps strength ≥90% symmetry, hop test battery ≥90% symmetry with good movement quality, ACL-RSI score ≥76, and completion of sport-specific training phases without adverse response. Athletes failing any criterion require targeted intervention before progressing.

The testing battery should be repeated rather than viewed as a single checkpoint. Many athletes demonstrate day-to-day variation in performance, and a single poor test result may not reflect true capacity. However, consistently failing criteria indicates inadequate preparation regardless of timeline pressure.

Documentation of specific deficits allows targeted intervention and re-testing, moving away from pass/fail approaches toward individualised preparation strategies.

Clinical Implementation

Implementing evidence-based return-to-sport testing requires systematic approaches that fit within clinical workflows. Many practices struggle with the time and resource demands of comprehensive testing, leading to abbreviated protocols that may miss critical deficits.

Standardised testing sessions, scheduled at appropriate intervals throughout rehabilitation, provide better outcomes than ad-hoc assessments. This approach allows longitudinal tracking of progress and identification of plateaus requiring intervention adjustment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the minimum strength requirement for ACL return-to-sport clearance?

Current evidence supports a minimum quadriceps strength symmetry of 90% between limbs, though this should be combined with hop testing and psychological readiness rather than used in isolation. Peak torque alone may miss deficits in rate of force development.

How reliable are hop tests for predicting re-injury risk?

The four-hop protocol (single hop, triple hop, crossover hop, 6-metre timed hop) with ≥90% symmetry and good movement quality significantly reduces re-injury likelihood. Movement quality assessment during testing may be more predictive than distance measures alone.

Why do some athletes re-injure despite passing physical tests?

Psychological factors, particularly fear of re-injury and reduced confidence, are strong predictors of re-injury risk. Athletes scoring below 76 on the ACL-RSI psychological readiness scale show higher re-injury rates regardless of physical test performance.
