Why 43% of Physiotherapy Care Is Non-Recommended — and What Clinics Can Do About It

A significant portion of physiotherapy care delivered across healthcare systems doesn't align with clinical practice guidelines. Studies examining adherence to evidence-based recommendations consistently reveal gaps between what research supports and what happens in clinical practice. This disconnect has real consequences for patient outcomes, resource allocation, and the profession's credibility with commissioners and insurers.

Understanding why these gaps exist and how to address them is critical for any clinic serious about delivering consistent, effective care. The solution isn't more training or better intentions — it's infrastructure that makes evidence-based practice the default option rather than the exception.

The Evidence Base Problem in Daily Practice

Clinical practice guidelines exist across virtually every area of physiotherapy practice. Comprehensive recommendations cover everything from neck and low back pain management12 to specialised interventions for conditions like adhesive capsulitis13 and complex regional pain syndrome8. These guidelines synthesise thousands of research studies into actionable protocols that should drive clinical decision-making.

The problem isn't the absence of evidence — it's the translation of that evidence into consistent clinical practice. Guidelines for managing lumbar spinal stenosis, for example, provide clear recommendations about exercise prescription, manual therapy application, and outcome measurement7. Yet clinic-to-clinic variation in applying these recommendations remains substantial.

This variation becomes particularly problematic when treating common conditions where evidence-based protocols are well-established. Take shoulder pain management: despite clear guidelines on assessment approaches and intervention strategies3, treatment approaches can vary dramatically between clinicians seeing similar presentations. The issue isn't clinical judgement — it's the lack of standardised tools to ensure guidelines are consistently applied.

Why Subjective Assessment Undermines Guideline Adherence

Most physiotherapy assessment relies heavily on subjective interpretation of strength, range of motion, and functional capacity. This creates two fundamental problems that contribute to non-recommended care: inconsistent baseline measurements and variable treatment progression decisions.

When initial assessments lack objective standardisation, treatment plans naturally deviate from evidence-based protocols. A patient presenting with chronic shoulder pain might receive completely different interventions depending on how their clinician subjectively interprets their strength deficits or movement patterns. This variation continues throughout treatment, with progression decisions based on clinical impression rather than measurable change.

The consequences compound over time. Without objective measures to track progress, clinicians often continue interventions longer than guidelines recommend or change approaches based on incomplete information. Evidence-based protocols for conditions like functional motor disorders provide specific guidance on treatment duration and progression criteria5, but these become meaningless without reliable measurement to determine when criteria are met.

Consider fall prevention protocols for older adults, where clinical guidelines provide detailed recommendations for assessment and intervention11. Without standardised strength and balance measurement, clinicians struggle to identify which patients meet criteria for specific interventions, leading to over-treatment of some patients and under-treatment of others.

The Hidden Costs of Clinical Variation

Non-recommended care creates costs that extend far beyond individual treatment sessions. When treatments don't follow evidence-based protocols, patients experience longer recovery times, increased risk of re-injury, and higher overall treatment costs. These outcomes reflect directly on clinic reputation and referral patterns.

From a business perspective, clinical variation makes resource planning nearly impossible. If treatment duration and intervention intensity vary significantly between clinicians treating similar conditions, clinics cannot accurately predict caseload capacity or staffing requirements. This inefficiency ultimately affects profitability and patient access to care.

The problem becomes more acute when dealing with specialised populations. Paediatric conditions like congenital muscular torticollis have specific protocols with clear timeframes and progression criteria4. Deviation from these guidelines often means prolonged treatment with diminishing returns, affecting both patient outcomes and clinic efficiency.

Insurance and commissioning bodies increasingly scrutinise treatment patterns and outcomes. Clinics that consistently deviate from evidence-based protocols face questions about clinical effectiveness and value for money. This scrutiny will only increase as healthcare systems demand greater accountability for resource allocation.

Evidence-Based Practice Requires Infrastructure, Not Just Knowledge

The assumption that clinical variation stems from knowledge gaps misses the fundamental issue. Most physiotherapists are familiar with relevant guidelines for their area of practice. The problem is that subjective assessment tools make it difficult to apply guidelines consistently, even when clinicians want to follow them.

Consider TENS application for acute and chronic pain management. Recent comprehensive analysis provides clear recommendations about appropriate candidates, treatment parameters, and expected outcomes9. Yet without objective measurement of pain levels and functional improvement, clinicians struggle to apply these recommendations systematically.

The same pattern appears across speciality areas. Guidelines for cerebral palsy management in children provide detailed protocols for intervention selection and progression6, but successful implementation requires objective measurement tools to identify appropriate candidates and track meaningful change. Without this infrastructure, even well-intentioned clinicians drift away from evidence-based protocols.

Effective implementation requires systems that make guideline adherence easier than deviation. This means objective assessment tools integrated into standard workflows, not additional administrative burden. When measurement becomes routine, guideline-based decision-making becomes routine.

Practical Steps to Reduce Clinical Variation

Addressing non-recommended care requires systematic changes to how clinics approach assessment and treatment planning. The goal isn't to restrict clinical judgement but to provide reliable data that supports evidence-based decision-making.

Start by identifying your clinic's most common conditions and reviewing relevant clinical practice guidelines. Hip fracture rehabilitation, for instance, has well-established protocols for assessment timing, intervention selection, and discharge criteria2. Map these guidelines against your current practices to identify variation points.

Implement standardised assessment protocols that generate objective data for treatment planning. This doesn't require expensive equipment — reliable measurement tools can be software-enabled rather than hardware-dependent. The key is consistency across all clinicians and all patients with similar presentations.

  • Establish baseline measurements for all patients using standardised tools
  • Create treatment progression criteria based on measurable change rather than clinical impression
  • Document adherence to evidence-based protocols as part of routine clinical notes
  • Review treatment patterns regularly to identify areas where variation exceeds clinical justification

Focus on conditions where your clinic sees high volume and where evidence-based protocols are well-established. Success in these areas creates momentum for broader implementation while generating immediate improvements in clinical consistency.

Moving Toward Consistent, Evidence-Based Care

Reducing the proportion of non-recommended care in your clinic isn't about following rigid protocols — it's about creating infrastructure that supports consistent application of evidence-based practice. When objective measurement becomes routine, clinicians can focus on interpretation and clinical reasoning rather than trying to establish reliable baselines through subjective assessment.

The physiotherapy profession's credibility with commissioners, insurers, and other healthcare providers depends on demonstrating consistent effectiveness. Clinics that can show systematic adherence to evidence-based protocols, supported by objective outcome measurement, position themselves as reliable partners in integrated care pathways.

This shift requires investment in measurement infrastructure, but the returns appear quickly in improved patient outcomes, more efficient resource utilisation, and stronger relationships with referral sources. More importantly, it ensures that the evidence base developed through decades of research actually reaches the patients who need it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my clinic's treatment approaches align with evidence-based guidelines?

Review clinical practice guidelines for your most common conditions and compare them with your actual treatment patterns. Look for variation in treatment duration, intervention selection, and progression criteria between similar patient presentations.

What's the difference between clinical judgement and non-recommended care?

Clinical judgement involves making evidence-informed decisions based on patient-specific factors. Non-recommended care occurs when treatment deviates from established protocols without clear clinical justification, often due to inconsistent assessment rather than deliberate clinical reasoning.

Can objective measurement tools actually reduce treatment variation between clinicians?

Yes, when clinicians have access to standardised, objective data about patient status and progress, their treatment decisions naturally align more closely with evidence-based protocols. The variation typically stems from inconsistent subjective assessment rather than different interpretations of reliable data.
