Why 43% of Physiotherapy Care Is Non-Recommended — and What Clinics Can Do About It

The numbers are stark: up to 43% of physiotherapy interventions delivered in clinical practice don't align with evidence-based recommendations. This isn't a failure of individual clinicians — it's a systemic problem rooted in measurement uncertainty and treatment variation that costs the NHS millions while potentially compromising patient outcomes.

Understanding why this gap exists and what clinics can do about it requires examining the measurement tools physiotherapists currently use, the evidence base that guides treatment decisions, and the role standardised assessment plays in bridging the gap between research and practice.

The Scale of Non-Recommended Care in Physiotherapy

Treatment variation in physiotherapy extends far beyond personal preference or clinical experience. When researchers examine rehabilitation protocols across different conditions, the inconsistencies become apparent. For anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction, systematic reviews reveal significant variation in exercise selection and progression criteria (Fontanier et al., 2025). Similarly, rotator cuff repair protocols show substantial differences in timing, load progression, and outcome measures used to guide treatment decisions (Bandara et al., 2021).

This variation matters because it directly impacts patient outcomes. Studies comparing standardised protocols to usual care consistently demonstrate superior results when treatment follows evidence-based guidelines. Digital physiotherapy platforms that enforce protocol adherence have shown measurably better outcomes than conventional care for conditions like chronic shoulder pain (Pak et al., 2023).

The financial implications are equally significant. Non-recommended care typically means either over-treatment (extending therapy beyond clinical necessity) or under-treatment (failing to provide adequate intervention intensity). Both scenarios increase per-case costs while reducing treatment effectiveness.

Why Evidence-Based Protocols Aren't Being Followed

The gap between evidence and practice isn't caused by clinicians ignoring research. Three systematic barriers prevent consistent implementation of evidence-based care:

Measurement uncertainty dominates clinical decision-making. Over 90% of physiotherapists rely on subjective strength testing, making it impossible to apply research-based loading criteria. When studies specify "progress to 80% of uninjured limb strength", but clinicians can't reliably measure that threshold, protocol adherence becomes impossible.

Protocol complexity creates implementation challenges. Blood flow restriction training, for example, has strong evidence for accelerating rehabilitation outcomes (Hughes et al., 2017), but requires specific pressure calculations and monitoring that many clinics lack the tools to implement consistently.

Outcome measurement gaps prevent clinicians from knowing whether their treatments are working. Without standardised benchmarks, physiotherapists can't distinguish between normal recovery variation and treatment failure until problems become obvious — often too late for course correction.

The Cost of Clinical Variation

Treatment inconsistency creates cascading problems that extend beyond individual patient care. When 43% of interventions are non-recommended, clinic efficiency suffers through several mechanisms:

Resource allocation becomes unpredictable. Some patients receive excessive treatment while others are discharged prematurely, creating scheduling inefficiencies and inconsistent caseload management.

Clinical outcomes vary significantly between practitioners within the same clinic, making it impossible to benchmark performance or identify best practices internally.

Insurance and commissioning relationships suffer when outcomes can't be reliably predicted or demonstrated. Payers increasingly demand evidence of clinical effectiveness, which requires consistent measurement and documentation.

Staff development becomes problematic when there's no objective standard for comparing clinical decisions or measuring improvement in practitioner performance.

Evidence-Based Interventions That Require Standardised Measurement

Several high-evidence interventions remain underutilised specifically because they require objective measurement tools that most clinics lack:

Progressive loading protocols represent the gold standard for most musculoskeletal conditions but require precise strength measurement to implement safely and effectively. Research consistently shows superior outcomes when loading is progressed based on objective criteria rather than time-based protocols (Harput et al., 2020).

Bilateral strength comparison provides the most reliable benchmark for return-to-activity decisions, but fewer than 10% of physiotherapy clinics have tools to measure this accurately. This forces clinicians to rely on subjective assessment for critical safety decisions.

Functional movement screening can identify compensation patterns and movement dysfunction, but only when measured against normative data. Without standardised benchmarks, movement assessment becomes subjective interpretation rather than objective analysis.

Combined interventions often show superior outcomes to single-modality treatment. Studies demonstrate that combining corticosteroid injection with structured physiotherapy produces better results than either intervention alone (Hsieh et al., 2023), but this requires precise coordination and measurement of both components.

Building Measurement Infrastructure for Evidence-Based Care

Reducing the 43% non-compliance rate requires systematic changes to how physiotherapy clinics measure and monitor patient progress. This isn't about replacing clinical judgement — it's about giving clinicians the infrastructure to apply their expertise more consistently and defensibly.

Standardised strength testing provides the foundation for implementing load-based progression protocols. When clinicians can reliably measure bilateral strength differences and track changes over time, they can apply research-based criteria with confidence.

Objective outcome tracking enables real-time treatment adjustment. Rather than waiting for discharge to evaluate success, standardised measurement allows clinicians to identify treatment response early and modify interventions accordingly.

Benchmark comparison gives context to individual measurements. Knowing that a patient's strength is at the 30th percentile for their demographic provides different clinical information than a generic "weak" assessment.

Protocol adherence monitoring ensures that evidence-based interventions are implemented as researched. This includes tracking exercise progression, load increases, and criterion-based advancement through rehabilitation phases.

Making Evidence-Based Care Practical

The solution to non-recommended care isn't more education about research findings — it's better tools for implementing what we already know works. Clinics that have reduced treatment variation share several characteristics:

They use objective measurements to guide treatment decisions rather than relying solely on subjective assessment. This enables consistent application of evidence-based protocols regardless of clinician experience or confidence.

They measure outcomes systematically, creating internal benchmarks that help identify when treatments are working and when they need modification.

They standardise key decision points in common treatment pathways, reducing variation while preserving clinical judgement for complex cases.

Most importantly, they frame measurement not as additional administrative burden, but as the infrastructure that makes clinical expertise more effective and defensible.

Reducing the 43% rate of non-recommended care requires systematic change, not individual effort. When clinics invest in measurement infrastructure that supports evidence-based practice, they create conditions where following research recommendations becomes the easiest choice, not the most difficult one.
