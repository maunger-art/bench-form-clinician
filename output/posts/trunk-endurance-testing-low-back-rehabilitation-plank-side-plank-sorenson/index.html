Trunk Endurance Testing in Low Back Rehabilitation: What Plank, Side Plank, and Sorenson Tell You

Trunk endurance testing provides objective data about core stability and muscular function that subjective assessment cannot capture. The plank, side plank, and Sorenson tests form the foundation of trunk endurance measurement, offering standardised protocols that translate directly into clinical decision-making for low back pain management.

These three tests measure different components of trunk stability: the plank assesses anterior core endurance, the side plank evaluates lateral stability, and the Sorenson test measures posterior chain endurance. Together, they provide a comprehensive picture of trunk function that guides treatment selection and tracks progress with precision unavailable through manual muscle testing alone.

The Clinical Foundation for Trunk Endurance Testing

Low back pain affects trunk muscle function in predictable patterns. Patients typically develop compensatory movement strategies that create muscular imbalances, reduced endurance capacity, and altered motor control patterns. Traditional strength testing captures maximum voluntary contraction but misses the endurance component crucial for functional activities and injury prevention.

Research demonstrates that exercise interventions targeting trunk stability and endurance produce superior outcomes in chronic low back pain management (Hayden et al., 2021). Neuromuscular control and resistance training programmes specifically designed around trunk endurance principles show significant improvements in pain reduction and functional capacity (Farragher et al., 2024). The key lies in measuring baseline endurance capacity accurately to design appropriate interventions.

Trunk endurance testing addresses three critical clinical questions: Can the patient maintain adequate spinal stabilisation during functional demands? Which muscle groups require targeted intervention? How does endurance capacity change throughout treatment? These questions cannot be answered through range of motion testing or subjective pain scales alone.

Plank Test Protocol and Normative Values

The plank test measures anterior trunk endurance through sustained isometric contraction of the core stabilisers. The patient maintains a prone position supported on forearms and toes, with the body forming a straight line from head to heels. Timing begins when proper position is achieved and ends when form breaks down or the patient cannot continue.

Proper form requires neutral spine alignment, engaged abdominal muscles, and controlled breathing throughout the hold. Common form breakdowns include hip sagging, excessive hip elevation, forward head posture, and breath holding. The test ends immediately when any of these compensations occur, not when the patient experiences discomfort.

Normative values for healthy adults show significant variation by age and sex:

  • Men aged 20-29: 124-180 seconds
  • Women aged 20-29: 83-120 seconds
  • Men aged 40-49: 72-115 seconds
  • Women aged 40-49: 60-90 seconds
  • Men aged 60+: 45-75 seconds
  • Women aged 60+: 35-60 seconds

These benchmarks provide context for interpreting individual results, but clinical significance depends more on bilateral comparisons and functional correlation than absolute values. A 45-year-old carpenter holding plank for 45 seconds may have adequate endurance for occupational demands, while a 25-year-old athlete with the same result likely needs intervention.

Side Plank Assessment and Lateral Stability

The side plank test evaluates lateral trunk endurance, specifically targeting the quadratus lumborum, obliques, and lateral hip stabilisers. This test requires greater neuromuscular coordination than the standard plank and often reveals asymmetries that correlate with movement dysfunction patterns.

Testing protocol involves side-lying position with the patient supported on one forearm and the side of the foot, maintaining a straight line from head to feet. Both sides must be tested independently to identify asymmetries. The non-supporting arm can rest on the hip or be placed across the chest for consistency.

Key performance indicators include maintenance of straight body alignment, stable shoulder position, and controlled breathing. Form breakdown typically manifests as hip dropping, shoulder rolling forward, or trunk rotation. These compensations often occur earlier on the affected side in patients with unilateral low back pain.

Expected hold times for side plank are typically 60-70% of standard plank performance. Asymmetries greater than 20% between sides warrant further investigation and targeted intervention. The side that fatigues first often corresponds to the side of pain or dysfunction, though this relationship is not absolute.

Sorenson Test and Posterior Chain Assessment

The Sorenson test measures posterior trunk endurance, specifically targeting the erector spinae, multifidus, and gluteal muscles. This test provides crucial data about the muscle groups most commonly affected in chronic low back pain and correlates strongly with functional lifting capacity.

Test positioning requires the patient prone on a treatment table with the upper body unsupported from the anterior superior iliac spines forward. The lower body is stabilised with straps or manual pressure, and the patient maintains horizontal position of the trunk with arms crossed over the chest.

Lumbar stabilisation exercises, which form the foundation of many low back pain treatment programmes, directly target the muscle groups assessed by the Sorenson test (Fontana et al., 2020). This makes the Sorenson particularly valuable for monitoring treatment response and adjusting exercise prescription based on measurable progress.

Normative values for the Sorenson test show considerable variation:

  • Healthy men: 146-240 seconds
  • Healthy women: 131-190 seconds
  • Chronic low back pain patients: typically 50-75% of healthy norms

Values below 60 seconds indicate significant posterior chain weakness requiring targeted intervention. The relationship between Sorenson performance and functional lifting capacity makes this test particularly relevant for return-to-work decisions and occupational rehabilitation planning.

Clinical Application and Treatment Implications

Trunk endurance test results guide treatment selection by identifying specific weaknesses and asymmetries. Patients with poor plank performance benefit from progressive anterior chain strengthening, while those with Sorenson deficits require posterior-focused interventions. Side plank asymmetries indicate targeted unilateral training needs.

The evidence base supports exercise interventions tailored to trunk endurance findings. Programmes incorporating specific trunk endurance training show superior outcomes compared to general exercise approaches (Hayden et al., 2021). The key lies in matching exercise prescription to measurable deficits rather than applying generic protocols.

Serial testing throughout treatment provides objective progress markers that supplement subjective pain reports. Patients often show measurable endurance improvements before reporting significant symptom changes, making these tests valuable for maintaining treatment compliance during early phases of recovery.

Treatment progression should target specific endurance thresholds based on functional demands. Office workers may achieve adequate function with modest improvements, while manual labourers require higher performance standards. The tests provide benchmarks for these decisions rather than relying solely on symptom resolution.

Integration with Comprehensive Assessment

Trunk endurance testing works best as part of comprehensive objective assessment rather than standalone measurement. Integration with movement screening, strength testing, and functional capacity evaluation provides complete clinical pictures that guide holistic treatment approaches.

The combination of objective trunk endurance data with evidence-based treatment protocols creates defensible clinical decision-making. Rather than adjusting treatment based on subjective reports alone, clinicians can modify interventions based on measurable performance changes that correlate with functional outcomes.

Documentation of baseline values, interim progress, and final outcomes provides clear evidence of treatment effectiveness. This objective data supports clinical reasoning, justifies treatment modifications, and demonstrates value to patients, referrers, and commissioners who increasingly demand measurable results from physiotherapy interventions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should trunk endurance tests be repeated during treatment?

Retest every 3-4 weeks during active treatment phases. This frequency allows sufficient time for measurable changes while providing regular progress markers to guide treatment modifications and maintain patient motivation.

What constitutes a clinically significant improvement in trunk endurance test scores?

Improvements of 25-30% from baseline values indicate clinically meaningful change. However, focus on functional correlation rather than absolute numbers - modest improvements may be highly significant for patients with severe initial deficits.

Can trunk endurance tests be used safely with acute low back pain patients?

These tests are most appropriate for subacute and chronic presentations. In acute phases, focus on basic movement assessment first. Introduce endurance testing when patients can perform basic movements without significant pain exacerbation.
